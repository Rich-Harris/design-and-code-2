    Mother Must Muzzle the Nuzzle

    By LINDA BAKERDEC. 4, 2005

    Inside

      Continue reading the main story Share This Page
      Continue reading the main story
      Continue reading the main story

      I'M in Powell's bookstore cafe, and the young couple next to me are canoodling: staring deeply into each other's eyes, holding hands across the table and smooching.

      I would say they're starting to annoy me, except, the thing is, I'm canoodling too, with my gangly, 65-pound, 10-year-old son.

      It started out very innocently. He was reading Asterix and Obelix and making an unholy mess out of a chocolate chunk cookie. I was reading the newspaper and sipping a cappuccino. But before I knew what was happening, he was seated firmly on my lap, hands thrust into my hair, and I was stroking his cheek and punctuating my caresses with tiny kisses.

      Now it's the young couple next to us who look annoyed.

      But what can I say? I'm finding it difficult to keep my hands off my son. And frankly the feeling is mutual.

      Much has been written about the empty-nest syndrome: the loss parents feel when their kids leave for college. Far less ink has been spilled over an equally challenging rite of passage: the time when mothers (and fathers) are supposed to cut back on the physical contact that is such an integral part of early child rearing.

      And I realize that, at some point, cut back I must. In restaurants I often feel as if my son and I are participating in an elaborate courtship ritual, one that is better suited to the one-horned rhinoceros or some such species that has yet to place a premium on good table manners. Resembling a wayward boyfriend, my son reaches for my hair. I slap his hand away, then I fondle his arm, face, shoulder.

      Oh, how I adore him.

      On a recent weekday evening I was saying goodbye to several friends when my son hurled himself across the living room and jumped into my arms, looking for all the world like a gorilla wrapped around a fire hydrant. I staggered backward, regained my balance, then struggled with the competing desires to push him away and hold him close.

      "Wow," a friend exclaimed. "He's so big. You still carry him around?"

      Then there was the time at the after-school pickup when my son rushed out of his classroom, grabbed a fistful of my hair and buried his mouth in my neck. The other parents, standing with their arms strewn lightly around their children's shoulders, smiled politely and moved a few paces away.

      Here's the reality. I love to be physical with my son, and with my 8-year-old daughter too. I've been stroking and kissing them since the day they were born. And now I'm supposed to stop? Cold turkey? What kind of puritanical culture do we live in?

      And that's not the half of it. If we Americans are prudish, we are also prurient, a binary opposition that leaves little room for the complex sensual attraction I feel for my son and daughter. When it comes to physical affection outside of the sexual, we are curiously limited in vocabulary and imagination.

      The United States isn't Italy, where heterosexual men walk down the street holding hands or linking arms. Nor do we possess the behavioral syntax to interpret, or emulate, the exuberant kisses strangers in other countries bestow on one another in greeting.

      Perhaps it's no surprise that our culture offers little space to articulate a theory and practice of maternal desire. (Thanks for the psychologist Daphne DeMarneffe, who wrote the book "Maternal Desire" last year, although her emphasis is on the emotional and intellectual satisfaction associated with mothering.) As far as I can tell, the notion of physical maternal pleasure has gone underground, kind of like samizdat.

      Despite the many conversations I've had with friends about poop, breast milk and other bodily fluids stemming from the messy practice of raising young children, I've yet to extemporize on the possibility of maintaining a corporeal relationship with my 10-year-old son. It's the kind of love, I guess, that dares not speak its name.

      Alas, I'm not much of a rebel. Channeling a cultural nightmare, I entertain the occasional worries about playing Jocasta to my son's Oedipus, a Gertrude to his Hamlet. I recall the mouth-on-mouth kiss Meryl Streep gave her brainwashed adult son, played by Liev Schreiber in the 2004 remake of "The Manchurian Candidate." As a mother her character is not a model of success. Not only does she implant her son with a microchip turning him into a cold-blooded killer, she's incestuous to boot.

      There's more. I think about all the women I know who have suffered the fallout from relationships with "mama's boys." As a wise friend once put it, the political assassin isn't the only personality profile to spring Medusa-like from dysfunctional mother-son relationships.

      I resolve to be part of the solution, not the problem.

      SO one day, after a particularly unpleasant experience involving a bus stop, filial limbs entangled with my own, and a grandmotherly bystander ("How old is your son, dear?" she inquired sweetly. "Nine," I prevaricated), I decide it's time to assert control.

      I am the adult in this parent-child equation, I tell myself. I must sever the physical bonds that have defined my relationship with my kids through childbirth and breast-feeding, diaper changing and co-sleeping, soothing scraped knees and toilet training. My son is growing older. I need to confront reality and facilitate his development into an independent human being.

      At dinner I announce my new plan to the entire family. My children, I explain, may give me a maximum of two kisses at a time, only on the cheek. There will be no full body hugs, no sitting on laps, no lying on the couch hip to thigh.

      My list of demands is met by shocked silence. I feel as if I am a speaker for an abstinence organization, spreading the good word about forgoing the pleasures of the flesh.

      Finally my daughter breaks the tension. "What about your arm, Mama?"

      Since she was a baby, my daughter has always found solace and respite from the world by stroking my forearm. A few years ago my husband took a couple of pictures of me balancing her on my hip. In the first image my daughter's mouth is latched onto my arm as if she were breast-feeding. In the second she is stroking the wet spot, her eyes glazed in reverie.

      I lock eyes with my precious daughter. I feel a wave of weakness. But I do not relent.

      I'm determined, in the days ahead, to practice what I preach. I find it's easier to crack down when my own parents visit. In an effort to model normative standards of maternal intimacy, I greet my mother with a chaste peck on the cheek and give my father a friendly pat on the back. When my son attempts to body slam me, I am the perfect disciplinarian.

      "Hands off my body," I say sternly. "This is unacceptable behavior."

      He glowers but backs off.

      The next couple of weeks prove stressful for everyone. "Get your mouth off Mommy's hair," my husband yells at my son every night at the dinner table. At the playground my daughter falls off the monkey bars and wails, "I want your arm, Mama, your arm!"

      When my son relapses, and I feel his mouth on my hair again, as he pushes his way into my arms while I'm reading on the couch, I immediately resort to the twin specters of bad parenting: shame and ridicule. "What will your friends say when they see you crawling all over me? Won't you be embarrassed -- at your age?"

      MY friends approve and disapprove of my tactics. "You really shouldn't humiliate him," offers one not so helpfully (she who breast-fed her son until he was 4 but appears to have severed all physical ties).

      "He'll grow out of it," says another.

      But what about me? At what point will my desire begin to wane? When will I stop taking such delight in my children's bodies: the perfect legs, the arms, that hair, those eyes?

      I page through assorted parenting books, to no avail. The gurus of attachment parenting -- experts, like Dr. William Sears, who advocate close physical contact throughout the preschool years -- tend to bring the discussion to a halt once the child turns 5 or 6. As a result parents of older kids are left to navigate a void. I'm drowning in maternal tristesse. I look forward to the diminishing of desire.

      On bad days I wonder if I am destined to become one of those mothers who can never let go of her adult sons, one of those awful, smothering women caricatured on stage and screen. I imagine myself as the evil (and aged) mother-in-law who eyes her female nemesis with jealousy, fear and a taste for battle, as in the 1998 film "Hush," in which Gwyneth Paltrow plays a young wife terrorized by an unhinged mother-in-law (played by a beleaguered Jessica Lange).

      On good days I spin my lingering maternal pleasures more positively. I am, I tell myself, an intelligent, compassionate and disciplined woman who also wants to remain physically affectionate with her growing children. Will I eventually have to sublimate that desire? Of course. I know the physical boundaries between me and my kids will never again be as permeable as they are today or were in the past.

      Filial independence comes at a cost. One afternoon my son and I will be sitting in a bookstore cafe, I sipping my cappuccino and skimming the newspaper while he gulps hot chocolate and reads "The Catcher in the Rye."

      I'll reach out to stroke his hair. "Mom," he'll say. "Please stop."

      And stop I will. But on that day I will weep rivers of tears.

      MODERN LOVE Linda Baker is a journalist in Portland, Ore.
